# From Tax Residency to Network Residency

A strategic position paper on the tax jurisdictional development path of network societies, from individual tax compliance to recognised represented collective status.

*Full paper to be published May 2026. This is a summary of the core argument.*

## Thesis

The next meaningful development in international tax is not confined to corporate
groups. It is the organised economic mass of highly mobile individuals acting
through network societies.

Existing international tax architecture, including BEPS Pillar One and Pillar
Two, assumes the multinational enterprise is the dominant optimisation unit.
That frame is becoming inadequate. As network societies aggregate economically
significant populations across jurisdictions, the relevant unit increasingly
becomes the organised transnational community itself.

The endpoint is the recognition of a non-geographic resident class whose tax
affairs, for defined income categories, are mediated collectively through the
network-society entity by status agreement with host jurisdictions. 

That agreement does not require new international law. It can take the form of a 
domestic administrative arrangement, a reporting safe harbour with amnesties, or a delegated 
withholding framework negotiated with a willing host sovereign. Territory is one 
path. Status is the real innovation.

## Position relative to other models

Zone models such as Prospera attach differentiated tax treatment to a territorial 
perimeter. They are place-bound by design where the benefits are geo-locked 
and do not travel with the person. The Balaji Srinivasan network state sequence is similar: assemble online, acquire land, seek recognition through economic density and gaining leverage. 
Territory comes first, and status follows if it comes at all.

**This paper argues status-plus-territory, where territory is optional. The foundation is recognition of a coordinated population as a distinct tax base and administrative class.**

This paper proposes a different sequence. Network citizenry is the status and can be supported by territory 
but territory is not a prerequisite for status. The innovation is the 
recognition of a coordinated population as a distinct administrative class whose 
fiscal affairs can be mediated collectively, regardless of whether that population 
occupies a contiguous physical space. A network society that achieves this recognition does not 
need to own land to negotiate with sovereigns. It needs economic mass, physical mobility, institutional 
credibility, and a professional infrastructure capable of representing its members 
with the same rigour that any other organised economic actor brings to fiscal 
negotiation.

## Development sequence

- **V1** Individual tax residency inside host systems
- **V2** Coordinated population compliance with recurring factual patterns
- **V3** Institutional and professional infrastructure
- **V4** Economic recognition and targeted negotiation
- **V5** Collective fiscal counterparty
- **Beyond V5** Network residency as a recognised status class

## Why I am working on this now

In 2012, writing an international tax masters paper, I proposed a tax system for global citizens: report and contribute once to a global body, distributing funds to countries based on each person's physical footprint during the period. No separate filing in each jurisdiction, no atomised compliance across multiple systems for one life lived across borders. That paper was written before blockchain and network states exposed the tax frictions of people aggregating across borders at scale.

Network societies are operating at V1 and early V2 today. Members remain fully exposed to ordinary domestic tax laws in their citizenship and host jurisdictions. The economic behaviours that develop naturally in network societies with income earned across multiple jurisdictions, contributions to shared infrastructure, participation in crypto-native and tokenised economies, sit awkwardly with tax codes and bilateral treaties designed for a different era. The result is genuine uncertainty about how existing laws apply, compliance risks that give members significant anxiety, and outcomes that are often inequitable given the facts of how these people actually live and work. 

This is why credentialed public accountants with diverse cross-border expertise and peer-training need to be present in these communities now, representing members against  interpretations of domestic laws while building the largely automated compliance systems that demonstrate collective good faith. That good faith record is the foundation on which later negotiation for diplomatic recognition and  carve-out tax treatment will depend. The work of moving to later stages, building the institutional, professional, and administrative infrastructure that makes that negotiation possible, is the present task.

This paper sets out the sequence and design principles for that work.

## The necessary monetary architecture at V4 and V5

The V1-V5 sequence describes the fiscal and jurisdictional development path of a network society. The monetary base underneath that becomes crucial at V4 and V5.

A collective fiscal counterparty negotiating with host sovereigns on behalf of its members needs a unit of account that no sovereign controls. Without that, the network society is negotiating its members' tax affairs in the currency of the jurisdiction it is trying to gain independence from. The collective status innovation becomes a more efficient interface to the existing system, not an alternative to it.

Bitcoin is the unit of account this model depends on. The model requires Bitcoin to function as the reference standard against which all economic activity in the network is measured, not as a speculative asset held on a balance sheet. Every balance sheet, every contribution agreement, every fiscal negotiation carries a Bitcoin-denominated reference value alongside its nominal currency value. This is the composite fractionalized money approach: Bitcoin as the reference unit, operational currencies as the transactional medium, settlement on Bitcoin underneath.

Operational currencies include network-specific instruments functioning within the network economy across different chains and protocols. What determines how much human oversight any given monetary function requires is how far it sits from Bitcoin settlement. The further from Bitcoin's security model, the more trust assumptions and counterparty risk, and the more the human oversight layer needs to mirror Bitcoin's own 'public good' properties — decentralised, independent, verifiable, with no single point of capture.

Global credentialed public accountants (GCPAs) operate as nodes in a decentralised professional network to provide that oversight, calibrated to distance but not applied uniformly, and acting in the public interest independently of all institutions and states.

This is what makes a valuation committee of any alternative system redundant. Conservative recognition of assets is not a governance problem solved by structural independence and constitutional rules. It is an accounting problem solved by a credentialed profession operating within a trust-minimised monetary system.

The network society that tries to reach V5 without this underneath it has built collective fiscal capacity on a fiat basis, which is only a better-administered version of the old problem, not a solution to it that people come to network states for.
